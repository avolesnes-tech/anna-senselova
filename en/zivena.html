"We too feel the longing for something higher and have been endowed with spirit by God; let us not allow this greatest gift to wither, but cultivate it conscientiously."
— Elena Maróthy-Šoltésová

How Živena entered my story.

Among the embroideries and folk-costume dolls I found in my grandparents' cellar, I also came across two volumes of the old Živena journal — issues covering years 36–37 and 38. They had been carefully stored there, as though someone had deliberately set them aside for the future.

Two volumes of the Živena journal from 1938 and 1936–37

Thanks in part to these two Živena annals, I was transported back in time, and with each article I felt I was coming to know my ancestors' world a little more.

Once I began leafing through them, I could not put them down. Živena was a periodical published by the women's association of the same name. Reading articles on topics such as child-rearing, marriage, recipes and fashion, I had the sense that this journal could have been published today and would carry the same value for contemporary women as it did a hundred years ago — because, despite all that time has changed, our qualities, longings and responsibilities have altered very little. What fascinated me in those volumes were the guides to infant care, advice on raising children, debates about women's role in society, recipes, exercise columns and fashion pages. And it was in those fashion columns that I encountered the name Aranka Šenšelová Hubková — the sister-in-law of Anna Šenšelová. Another thread connecting my family to this world.

I also immediately found an article about embroideries. It was about women who travelled through Slovak villages collecting old embroideries and folk costumes. No names were given in the article, but in all likelihood these were women from Lipa — perhaps Anna Šenšelová herself among them. They were met with mockery: people told them there was nothing like that in their homes, that the costumes had long since been given to rag-collectors or burned. Who would wear such things today? As is the way with new trends, people were quick to dismiss the old, even when it had once been dear to them. This is how Western influences were making their way into Slovak households at the time, and traditional embroidery was slowly losing its place.

What was Živena

The oldest women's association in Slovakia.

Živena was founded on 4 August 1869 in Turčiansky Svätý Martin. At a time when Slovak women had no right to education in their mother tongue, a group of patriots and courageous women decided to act. The association's purpose was to educate Slovak women — in finance, cookery, child-rearing and culture. By the standards of the day, it was a revolutionary act.

At first glance, feminism and embroidery might seem to have little in common. The opposite is true. For Živena, embroidery was a tool of resistance — quiet, but effective. The association collected patterns, organised exhibitions and trained embroiderers. Not out of nostalgia, but out of a conviction that preserving tradition was an act of national defiance. And at the same time, an opportunity for women to earn their own income.

Education of women

Živena founded girls' schools and courses in cookery, hygiene, child-rearing and household management. In 1919 it opened its own school, and in 1922 the first nursery in Slovakia.

Preservation of folk art

From as early as 1887 it organised exhibitions of Slovak embroideries. From 1895 it systematically collected folk costumes in collaboration with the Slovak Museum Society.

Publishing

The Živena Almanac (1872), the journal Dennica (1898) and the association's literary journal Živena (1910) — the first women's media in Slovakia, where women wrote for women.

Economic independence

Živena worked closely with Lipa and gave women the chance to turn a traditional skill into real income. Embroidery became not only a cultural but also an economic tool.

Chronicle

More than 150 years.

Ten moments that shaped the oldest women's association in Slovakia.

Founded 1869

Živena is established in Martin. Anna Pivková becomes its first chairwoman. At the founding assembly, 73 members sign up immediately.

Resilience 1875

After the closure of Matica slovenská, Živena remains the only functioning Slovak association — the guardian of national identity.

Culture 1887

A landmark exhibition of Slovak embroideries in Martin — one of the first major public acts of preserving folk art.

Leadership 1894

Elena Maróthy-Šoltésová becomes chairwoman, a role she will hold until 1927. Under her leadership, Živena enters its most fruitful period.

Connection 1910

The Živena journal begins publication. That same year, at Šoltésová's initiative, Lipa is founded — and from that moment their fates are closely intertwined.

Flourishing 1919–22

Živena opens its own first school and the first nursery on Slovak territory. During the First Czechoslovak Republic the association has 90 local branches and more than 6,000 members.

Exhibition 1925

Živena's ethnographic exhibition in Banská Bystrica — one of the largest presentations of Slovak folk art and traditional dress of its era. A joint event with the Lipa association.

Dissolution 1955

Živena is forcibly dissolved by the communist regime.

Revival 1990

Živena is re-established thanks to a group of determined women led by the writer Hana Zelinová.

Present day Today

Živena brings together more than 1,000 members across 12 local branches throughout Slovakia. Since 2022 its chairwoman has been Alena Heribanová.
